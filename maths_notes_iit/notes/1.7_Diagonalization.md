## What is Diagonalization? (Layman's Explanation)

Imagine you have a complicated matrix $A$. Wouldn't it be nice if you could find a **simpler version** — a diagonal matrix $D$ — that behaves the same way as $A$? That's exactly what diagonalization does.

We find a matrix $P$ (made of eigenvectors) such that:

$$
\boxed{P^{-1}AP = D}
$$

Where $D$ is a diagonal matrix with **eigenvalues** on the diagonal.

**Why is this useful?**
- Computing $A^{100}$ is hard, but $D^{100}$ is easy (just raise each diagonal entry to 100th power)
- $A^n = PD^nP^{-1}$

---

## Similar Matrices

### Definition
Two matrices $A$ and $B$ are **similar** if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that:

$$
B = P^{-1}AP
$$

### Properties of Similar Matrices
If $A$ and $B$ are similar, they have:
1. Same **eigenvalues**
2. Same **characteristic polynomial**
3. Same **determinant**: $|A| = |B|$
4. Same **trace**: $\text{tr}(A) = \text{tr}(B)$
5. Same **rank**: $\rho(A) = \rho(B)$

**Important:** Similar matrices are essentially the "same transformation" viewed from different bases.

---

## When is a Matrix Diagonalizable?

### Theorem
An $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if and only if it has **$n$ linearly independent eigenvectors**.

### Sufficient Conditions (if any of these hold, $A$ is diagonalizable):
1. $A$ has $n$ **distinct eigenvalues** → always diagonalizable
2. For every eigenvalue, **geometric multiplicity = algebraic multiplicity** (GM = AM)
3. $A$ is a **real symmetric matrix** → always diagonalizable (and can be diagonalized by an orthogonal matrix)
4. $A$ is a **Hermitian matrix** → always diagonalizable (by a unitary matrix)

### When NOT Diagonalizable:
If for some eigenvalue, GM < AM, then $A$ is **not diagonalizable**.

**Example:** $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$ has $\lambda = 2$ (AM = 2) but only one eigenvector (GM = 1). NOT diagonalizable.

## How to Diagonalize a Matrix

### Step-by-Step Process

1. Find **eigenvalues** $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n$ by solving $|A - \lambda I| = 0$
2. Find **eigenvectors** $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ for each eigenvalue
3. Check that you have $n$ **linearly independent** eigenvectors
4. Form $P = [X_1 | X_2 | \cdots | X_n]$ (columns are eigenvectors)
5. Then $D = P^{-1}AP = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n)$

---

### Example 1: Diagonalize a 2×2 Matrix

Diagonalize $A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 1: Eigenvalues**

$|A - \lambda I| = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda-5)(\lambda-2) = 0$

$\lambda_1 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = 2$

**Step 2: Eigenvectors**

$\lambda = 5$: $(A-5I)X = 0$ → $X_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

$\lambda = 2$: $(A-2I)X = 0$ → $X_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 3:** Two distinct eigenvalues → two linearly independent eigenvectors ✓

**Step 4:** $P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$, $D = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 5: Verify:**

$P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-3}\begin{bmatrix} -2 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & -1/3 \end{bmatrix}$

$P^{-1}AP = \begin{bmatrix} 2/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & -1/3 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = D$ ✓

---

### Example 2: Diagonalize a 3×3 Matrix

Diagonalize $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 1:** $|A - \lambda I| = -\lambda^3 + 4\lambda^2 - 3\lambda = -\lambda(\lambda-1)(\lambda-3) = 0$

$\lambda_1 = 0$, $\lambda_2 = 1$, $\lambda_3 = 3$

**Step 2: Eigenvectors**

$\lambda = 0$: $AX = 0$:
$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}X = 0$

$R_2 \to R_2 + R_1$, $R_3$: reduces to $x = y = z = t$

$X_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

$\lambda = 1$: $(A - I)X = 0$:
$\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}X = 0$

$y = 0$, $x + z = 0$. Let $z = t$: $X_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$

$\lambda = 3$: $(A - 3I)X = 0$:
$\begin{bmatrix} -2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & -1 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}X = 0$

Reduces to: $x = t$, $y = -2t$, $z = t$

$X_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 3:** $P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & -2 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, $D = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

---

## Application: Computing $A^n$

If $A = PDP^{-1}$, then:

$$
A^n = PD^nP^{-1}
$$

Where $D^n = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^n, \lambda_2^n, \ldots, \lambda_k^n)$

### Example: Find $A^5$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

From Example 1: $A = PDP^{-1}$ where $P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$, $D = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

$A^5 = PD^5P^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 3125 & 0 \\ 0 & 32 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 2/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & -1/3 \end{bmatrix}$

## Orthogonal Matrices

### Definition
A real matrix $P$ is **orthogonal** if:
$$
P^T P = PP^T = I \implies P^{-1} = P^T
$$

### Properties
1. $|P| = \pm 1$
2. Rows (and columns) of $P$ form an **orthonormal set**
3. $P$ preserves lengths: $\|Px\| = \|x\|$
4. $P$ preserves angles and dot products

### Example
$$
P = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}
$$

$P^T P = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I$ ✓

---

## Unitary Matrices

### Definition
A complex matrix $U$ is **unitary** if:
$$
U^\theta U = UU^\theta = I \implies U^{-1} = U^\theta = \bar{U}^T
$$

(Conjugate transpose is the inverse.)

**Unitary is the complex version of orthogonal.**

### Properties
1. $|U| = \pm 1$ (or any complex number with $|$absolute value$| = 1$)
2. Eigenvalues have $|\lambda| = 1$
3. Rows (columns) form an orthonormal set in complex inner product

---

## Orthogonal Diagonalization (Diagonalization of Symmetric Matrices)

### Theorem (Spectral Theorem)
Every **real symmetric matrix** can be diagonalized by an **orthogonal matrix**:

$$
P^T AP = D
$$

Where $P$ is orthogonal (columns are orthonormal eigenvectors) and $D$ has eigenvalues on the diagonal.

### Steps for Orthogonal Diagonalization
1. Find eigenvalues of $A$
2. Find eigenvectors for each eigenvalue
3. If eigenvectors for the same eigenvalue are not orthogonal, use **Gram-Schmidt** process
4. **Normalize** each eigenvector (divide by its length to make unit vectors)
5. Form $P$ from these **orthonormal** eigenvectors as columns

---

### Example: Orthogonally Diagonalize

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}
$$

**Step 1: Eigenvalues**
$|A - \lambda I| = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda-3)(\lambda-1) = 0$

$\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 1$

**Step 2: Eigenvectors**
$\lambda = 3$: $X_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

$\lambda = 1$: $X_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 3: Check orthogonality**
$X_1 \cdot X_2 = 1(1) + 1(-1) = 0$ ✓ (Already orthogonal — eigenvectors of symmetric matrices with different eigenvalues are always orthogonal!)

**Step 4: Normalize**
$\hat{X}_1 = \frac{X_1}{\|X_1\|} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

$\hat{X}_2 = \frac{X_2}{\|X_2\|} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 5:**
$$
P = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$P^TAP = D$ ✓

## Diagonalization of Hermitian Matrices

### Definition Recall
A **Hermitian matrix** satisfies $A^\theta = A$ (conjugate transpose equals itself).

### Key Properties
1. All eigenvalues are **real**
2. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are **orthogonal** (in complex inner product)
3. Can always be diagonalized by a **unitary matrix**: $U^\theta A U = D$

### Example: Diagonalize the Hermitian matrix

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1+i \\ 1-i & 3 \end{bmatrix}
$$

**Verify Hermitian:** $A^\theta = \overline{A^T} = \overline{\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1-i \\ 1+i & 3 \end{bmatrix}} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1+i \\ 1-i & 3 \end{bmatrix} = A$ ✓

**Eigenvalues:**
$|A - \lambda I| = (2-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - (1+i)(1-i) = 0$

$(2-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = 0$ (since $(1+i)(1-i) = 1+1 = 2$)

$\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 4 = 0$

$(\lambda - 4)(\lambda - 1) = 0$

$\lambda_1 = 4$, $\lambda_2 = 1$ (both real ✓)

**Eigenvectors:**

$\lambda = 4$: $\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1+i \\ 1-i & -1 \end{bmatrix}X = 0$

$-2x_1 + (1+i)x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_1 = \frac{1+i}{2}x_2$

$X_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1+i \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$

$\lambda = 1$: $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1+i \\ 1-i & 2 \end{bmatrix}X = 0$

$x_1 + (1+i)x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_1 = -(1+i)x_2$

$X_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -(1+i) \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1-i \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Normalize** and form unitary matrix $U$ to get $U^\theta AU = D = \text{diag}(4, 1)$.

## Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalization Process

When eigenvectors are NOT orthogonal (happens with repeated eigenvalues), we use Gram-Schmidt.

### Process
Given linearly independent vectors $\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k\}$, create orthogonal vectors:

$$
u_1 = v_1
$$

$$
u_2 = v_2 - \frac{\langle v_2, u_1 \rangle}{\langle u_1, u_1 \rangle} u_1
$$

$$
u_3 = v_3 - \frac{\langle v_3, u_1 \rangle}{\langle u_1, u_1 \rangle} u_1 - \frac{\langle v_3, u_2 \rangle}{\langle u_2, u_2 \rangle} u_2
$$

Then **normalize** each: $\hat{u}_i = \frac{u_i}{\|u_i\|}$

### Example: Orthogonalize $\{(1, 1, 0), (1, 0, 1)\}$

$u_1 = (1, 1, 0)$

$u_2 = (1, 0, 1) - \frac{(1,0,1) \cdot (1,1,0)}{(1,1,0) \cdot (1,1,0)}(1, 1, 0) = (1, 0, 1) - \frac{1}{2}(1, 1, 0) = (1/2, -1/2, 1)$

Check: $u_1 \cdot u_2 = 1/2 - 1/2 + 0 = 0$ ✓

## Bilinear Form

### Definition
A **bilinear form** on a vector space $V$ over field $F$ is a function $f: V \times V \to F$ that is **linear in each variable separately**:

1. $f(\alpha u_1 + \beta u_2, v) = \alpha f(u_1, v) + \beta f(u_2, v)$
2. $f(u, \alpha v_1 + \beta v_2) = \alpha f(u, v_1) + \beta f(u, v_2)$

### Matrix Representation
Every bilinear form can be written as $f(X, Y) = X^T A Y$ for some matrix $A$.

---

## Quadratic Form

### Definition
A **quadratic form** in $n$ variables is a homogeneous polynomial of degree 2:

$$
Q(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij}x_i x_j = X^T A X
$$

Where $A$ is a **symmetric matrix** (we can always make it symmetric).

### Example
$Q(x, y, z) = 2x^2 + 3y^2 + z^2 + 4xy - 2xz + 6yz$

Matrix form: $Q = X^T A X$ where:
$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 3 & 3 \\ -1 & 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

(Diagonal = coefficients of squared terms; off-diagonal $a_{ij}$ = half the coefficient of $x_i x_j$)

### Classification by Eigenvalues

| All eigenvalues | Quadratic form is... |
|----------------|---------------------|
| All > 0 | **Positive definite** |
| All ≥ 0 | **Positive semi-definite** |
| All < 0 | **Negative definite** |
| All ≤ 0 | **Negative semi-definite** |
| Some positive, some negative | **Indefinite** |

### Reducing to Canonical Form
Using the substitution $X = PY$ (where $P$ diagonalizes $A$):

$Q = X^TAX = (PY)^TA(PY) = Y^T(P^TAP)Y = Y^TDY$

$= \lambda_1 y_1^2 + \lambda_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + \lambda_n y_n^2$

This is the **canonical form** — just sum of squares with eigenvalues as coefficients.

## Practice Questions

**Q1.** Diagonalize $A = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -2 & 2 \\ -2 & 3 & -1 \\ 2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ (symmetric matrix).

**Solution:**
$|A - \lambda I| = (\lambda - 2)^2(\lambda - 8) = 0$

$\lambda_1 = 2$ (AM = 2), $\lambda_2 = 8$ (AM = 1)

$\lambda = 2$: Solve $(A - 2I)X = 0$: $X_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$, $X_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

$\lambda = 8$: Solve $(A - 8I)X = 0$: $X_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

Orthogonalize $X_1, X_2$ (for repeated eigenvalue):
$X_1 \cdot X_2 = -1 + 0 + 0 = -1 \neq 0$ (not orthogonal)

Apply Gram-Schmidt: $X_2' = X_2 - \frac{X_2 \cdot X_1}{X_1 \cdot X_1}X_1 = (-1,0,1) - \frac{-1}{5}(1,2,0) = (-4/5, 2/5, 1)$

Normalize all three and form $P$. Then $P^TAP = \text{diag}(2, 2, 8)$.

---

**Q2.** Is $A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ diagonalizable?

**Solution:**
$\lambda = 3$ (repeated, AM = 2)

$(A - 3I)X = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}X = 0$ → only one eigenvector: $X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

GM = 1 < AM = 2 → **NOT diagonalizable**.

---

**Q3.** Classify the quadratic form $Q = x^2 + 2y^2 + z^2 + 2xy$.

**Solution:**
$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

$|A - \lambda I| = (1-\lambda)[(1-\lambda)(2-\lambda) - 1] = (1-\lambda)(\lambda^2 - 3\lambda + 1) = 0$

$\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = \frac{3+\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 2.618$, $\lambda_3 = \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 0.382$

All eigenvalues > 0 → **Positive definite**. ✓

## Key Formulas to Remember

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| $P^{-1}AP = D$ | Diagonalization |
| $A^n = PD^nP^{-1}$ | Power of matrix |
| $P^TAP = D$ (symmetric $A$) | Orthogonal diagonalization |
| $U^\theta AU = D$ (Hermitian $A$) | Unitary diagonalization |
| GM = AM for all $\lambda$ | Condition for diagonalizability |
| Distinct eigenvalues → diagonalizable | Sufficient condition |
| $Q = X^TAX = Y^TDY$ | Canonical form of quadratic form |
