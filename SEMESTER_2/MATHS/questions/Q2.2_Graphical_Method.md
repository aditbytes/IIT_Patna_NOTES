# Questions & Solutions: 2.2 Graphical Method

> **Topic:** Graphical Solution of LPP, Corner Points, Bounded/Unbounded, Multiple Optima, Infeasible
> **Level:** Easy → Hard

---

## 🟢 Easy (Q1–Q9)

**Q1.** When is the graphical method applicable?

**Solution:**
The graphical method is applicable when an LPP has **exactly two decision variables** ($x$ and $y$), so the feasible region can be plotted on a 2D coordinate plane.

---

**Q2.** Solve: Maximize $Z=3x+2y$ s.t. $x+y\leq4$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Corner points: $(0,0)$, $(4,0)$, $(0,4)$.

| Point | $Z=3x+2y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(4,0)$ | 12 |
| $(0,4)$ | 8 |

**Maximum $Z=12$ at $(4,0)$.**

---

**Q3.** Sketch $x+y\leq4$ and identify its feasible half-plane.

**Solution:**
The line $x+y=4$ passes through $(4,0)$ and $(0,4)$. The feasible half-plane is the region **below and including** the line (where $x+y\leq4$), which includes the origin since $0+0=0\leq4$ ✓.

---

**Q4.** How do you identify the corner points of a feasible region?

**Solution:**
Corner points (vertices) are found by: solving each pair of boundary lines simultaneously, then checking which intersection points satisfy **all** constraints.

---

**Q5.** What does it mean if the feasible region is empty?

**Solution:**
If no point satisfies all constraints simultaneously, the feasible region is **empty/infeasible**. The LPP has **no solution** — it is an **infeasible problem**.

---

**Q6.** Solve: Maximize $Z=x+y$ s.t. $x\leq3$, $y\leq3$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Corner points: $(0,0)$, $(3,0)$, $(3,3)$, $(0,3)$.

$Z$ at $(3,3)=6$. **Maximum $Z=6$ at $(3,3)$.**

---

**Q7.** What are iso-profit lines in the graphical method?

**Solution:**
**Iso-profit lines** are lines of the form $Z = c_1x + c_2y = k$ (constant $k$). Moving these lines in the direction of increasing $k$ (for maximization) until the last point of contact with the feasible region gives the optimum.

---

**Q8.** Solve: Minimize $Z=x+2y$ s.t. $x+y\geq3$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Feasible region: $x+y\geq3$, $x,y\geq0$ — unbounded above.

Corner point: $(3,0)$ and $(0,3)$.

$Z(3,0)=3$, $Z(0,3)=6$.

Since region is unbounded (values go to infinity in +direction), no maximum. **Minimum $Z=3$ at $(3,0)$.**

---

**Q9.** Find corner points of: $x+y\leq6$, $x\leq4$, $y\leq4$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
- $(0,0)$: Origin.
- $(4,0)$: $x=4$, $y=0$.
- $(4,2)$: $x=4$, $x+y=6 \implies y=2$.
- $(2,4)$: $y=4$, $x+y=6 \implies x=2$.
- $(0,4)$: $x=0$, $y=4$.

Check all satisfy constraints ✓. Five corner points.

## 🟡 Medium (Q10–Q18)

**Q10.** Maximize $Z=5x+4y$ s.t. $6x+4y\leq24$, $x+2y\leq6$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Corner points:

$6x+4y=24$, $y=0$: $x=4$. → $(4,0)$
$x+2y=6$, $x=0$: $y=3$. → $(0,3)$
Intersection: $6x+4y=24$ and $x+2y=6$. Multiply 2nd by 2: $2x+4y=12$. Subtract: $4x=12\implies x=3$, $y=3/2$.→ $(3, 1.5)$

| Point | $Z=5x+4y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(4,0)$ | 20 |
| $(3,1.5)$ | 15+6=**21** |
| $(0,3)$ | 12 |

**Maximum $Z=21$ at $(3,1.5)$.**

---

**Q11.** Solve: Minimize $Z=3x+5y$ s.t. $x+3y\geq3$, $x+y\geq2$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Boundary intersections:
- $x+y=2$, $y=0$: $(2,0)$.
- $x+3y=3$, $x=0$: $(0,1)$.
- Intersection: $x+3y=3$ and $x+y=2$: subtract → $2y=1\implies y=1/2$, $x=3/2$.

Corner points (of bounded part): $(2,0)$, $(1.5,0.5)$, $(0,1)$.

| Point | $Z=3x+5y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(2,0)$ | 6 |
| $(1.5,0.5)$ | 4.5+2.5=**7** |
| $(0,1)$ | 5 |

Wait — also check $(3,0)$ since $x+3(0)=3\geq3$ ✓, but $3+0=3\geq2$ ✓. $Z(3,0)=9$. 

Re-evaluating: the infimum is at... $(2,0)$: $Z=6$. $(0,1)$: $Z=5$. Corner $(0,1)$: check $0+3(1)=3\geq3$ ✓, $0+1=1<2$ ✗. Not feasible.

Feasible corners: $(2,0)$, $(1.5,0.5)$, $(0,3)$. 
$Z(0,3)=15$. **Minimum $Z=6$ at $(2,0)$.**

---

**Q12.** What is multiple optima? Give an example.

**Solution:**
**Multiple optima** occur when the objective function's iso-profit line is **parallel** to one side of the feasible polygon. All points on that edge are optimal.

**Example:** Maximize $Z=x+y$ s.t. $x+y\leq4$, $x,y\geq0$. The line $Z=x+y=4$ coincides with the boundary → entire edge from $(4,0)$ to $(0,4)$ is optimal. Maximum $Z=4$ achieved by infinitely many points.

---

**Q13.** Solve graphically: Maximize $Z=4x+3y$ s.t. $2x+y\leq14$, $x+y\leq8$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
- $2x+y=14$, $y=0$: $(7,0)$.
- $x+y=8$, $x=0$: $(0,8)$.
- Intersection: $2x+y=14$, $x+y=8$: subtract → $x=6$, $y=2$.

| Point | $Z=4x+3y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(7,0)$ | 28 |
| $(6,2)$ | 24+6=**30** |
| $(0,8)$ | 24 |

**Maximum $Z=30$ at $(6,2)$.**

**Q14.** Identify infeasible LPP: $x+y\geq6$, $x+y\leq4$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
We need $x+y\geq6$ AND $x+y\leq4$ simultaneously. This requires $6\leq x+y\leq4$, which is impossible (since $6>4$).

**Feasible region is empty → LPP is infeasible.** No solution exists.

**Q15.** Solve: Maximize $Z=x+y$ s.t. $x-y\leq2$, $x+y\leq4$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Corner points:
- $x-y=2$, $y=0$: $(2,0)$.
- $x+y=4$, $x=0$: $(0,4)$.
- Intersection: $x-y=2$ and $x+y=4$: $2x=6\implies x=3,y=1$.

| Point | $Z=x+y$ |
|-------|---------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(2,0)$ | 2 |
| $(3,1)$ | **4** |
| $(0,4)$ | 4 |

Multiple optima: entire edge from $(3,1)$ to $(0,4)$ gives $Z=4$. **Maximum $Z=4$.**

**Q16.** Minimize $Z=2x+y$ s.t. $x+y\geq5$, $x+2y\geq8$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Intersection: $x+y=5$ and $x+2y=8$: subtract → $y=3$, $x=2$. → $(2,3)$.
- $y=0$, $x+2(0)=8$: $(8,0)$.
- $x=0$, $0+y=5$: $(0,5)$.

| Point | $Z=2x+y$ |
|-------|---------|
| $(8,0)$ | 16 |
| $(2,3)$ | **7** |
| $(0,5)$ | 5 |

Wait: $(0,5)$: $0+5=5\geq5$ ✓, $0+10=10\geq8$ ✓. $Z=5$.
$(2,3)$: $Z=7$. The minimum is at $(0,5)$? Let me check: As we move left from $(8,0)$, $Z$ decreases. At $(0,5)$: $Z=5$. Continue along $x=0$, $y$ increases: $Z=y$ increases. So minimum is at $(0,5)$.

Actually check $(0,5)$ vs $(2,3)$: $5<7$, so **Minimum $Z=5$ at $(0,5)$.**

**Q17.** Find the maximum of $Z=3x+2y$ over the feasible region with vertices $(0,0)$, $(5,0)$, $(4,3)$, $(0,4)$.

**Solution:**
| Point | $Z=3x+2y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(5,0)$ | **15** |
| $(4,3)$ | 12+6=18 |
| $(0,4)$ | 8 |

Wait: $(4,3)$ gives $Z=18 > 15$. **Maximum $Z=18$ at $(4,3)$.**

**Q18.** When does a maximization LPP have an unbounded solution?

**Solution:**
A maximization LPP has an **unbounded solution** (objective $\to+\infty$) when:
1. The feasible region is **unbounded** in the direction of increasing $Z$.
2. Specifically, when the feasible region extends infinitely in a direction where the objective function keeps increasing.

Example: Maximize $Z=x+y$ s.t. $x\geq0$, $y\geq0$ — $Z$ can be made arbitrarily large.

## 🔴 Hard (Q19–Q25)

**Q19.** Solve: Maximize $Z=6x_1+4x_2$ s.t. $2x_1+3x_2\leq120$, $2x_1+x_2\leq80$, $x_1\leq30$, $x_1,x_2\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Find corner points:
- $x_1=0$, $y=0$: $(0,0)$.
- $x_1=30$, $y=0$: $(30,0)$. Check: $60\leq120$✓, $60\leq80$✓.
- $x_1=30$, $2x_1+x_2=80\implies x_2=20$: $(30,20)$. Check: $60+60=120\leq120$✓.
- Intersect $2x_1+3x_2=120$ and $2x_1+x_2=80$: subtract → $2x_2=40\implies x_2=20$, $x_1=30$. Same point!
- $x_1=0$, $2(0)+3x_2=120\implies x_2=40$: $(0,40)$.

| Point | $Z=6x_1+4x_2$ |
|-------|--------------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(30,0)$ | 180 |
| $(30,20)$ | **260** |
| $(0,40)$ | 160 |

**Maximum $Z=260$ at $(30,20)$.**

**Q20.** An LPP has feasible region with vertices $(1,2)$, $(3,4)$, $(4,1)$, $(2,0)$. Maximize $Z=ax+by$. For what $a,b>0$ is $(3,4)$ the unique optimum?

**Solution:**
$(3,4)$ must give strictly higher $Z$ than all other vertices:
- $3a+4b > a+2b \implies 2a+2b>0$ ✓ (always true for $a,b>0$)
- $3a+4b > 4a+b \implies 3b>a \implies a<3b$
- $3a+4b > 2a+0b \implies a+4b>0$ ✓

Condition: $a < 3b$, i.e., $\frac{a}{b} < 3$.

**Q21.** Use iso-profit line method: Maximize $Z=3x+5y$ s.t. $x\leq4$, $y\leq6$, $3x+2y\leq18$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Corner points: $(0,0)$, $(4,0)$, $(4,3)$ [from $3(4)+2y=18\implies y=3$], $(2,6)$ [from $3x+2(6)=18\implies x=2$], $(0,6)$.

| Point | $Z=3x+5y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(4,0)$ | 12 |
| $(4,3)$ | 12+15=27 |
| $(2,6)$ | 6+30=**36** |
| $(0,6)$ | 30 |

**Maximum $Z=36$ at $(2,6)$.**

Iso-profit method: Draw $3x+5y=k$ for increasing $k$; the last contact with feasible region is at $(2,6)$.

**Q22.** Solve: Minimize $Z=x+2y$ s.t. $x+2y\geq10$, $3x+y\geq12$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Intersection of $x+2y=10$ and $3x+y=12$:
Multiply 2nd by 2: $6x+2y=24$. Subtract 1st: $5x=14\implies x=2.8$, $y=(10-2.8)/2=3.6$.

Corner points:
- $(0,12)$: $x=0,3(0)+y=12$. $Z=0+24=24$.
- $(2.8,3.6)$: $Z=2.8+7.2=10$.
- $(10,0)$: $x=10,y=0$. $Z=10$.

**Minimum $Z=10$ at $(2.8,3.6)$ and also at $(10,0)$! Both give $Z=10$.** Multiple optima.

Verify: $Z=x+2y=x+2y$ on line $x+2y=10$, so entire segment from $(10,0)$ to $(2.8,3.6)$ is optimal.

**Q23.** Construct an LPP with exactly two optimal corner points. Solve it graphically.

**Solution:**
**Construction:** Maximize $Z=x+y$ s.t. $x+y\leq5$, $x\leq4$, $y\leq4$, $x,y\geq0$.

Corner points: $(0,0),(4,0),(4,1),(1,4),(0,4)$ [where $x+y=5$ meets $x=4$ at $(4,1)$ and $y=4$ at $(1,4)$].

$Z(4,1)=5$, $Z(1,4)=5$, all others $<5$.

**Two optimal corners: $(4,1)$ and $(1,4)$**, both with $Z=5$. (All points on segment between them are also optimal — multiple optima.)

---

**Q24.** Prove that if $Z$ attains its maximum at two corner points, it attains the same maximum at all convex combinations of those points.

**Solution:**
Let $P^*,Q^*$ both give maximum $Z^*$. Any convex combination: $R=\lambda P^*+(1-\lambda)Q^*$, $\lambda\in[0,1]$.

$R$ is feasible (feasible region is convex). $Z(R)=Z(\lambda P^*+(1-\lambda)Q^*)=\lambda Z(P^*)+(1-\lambda)Z(Q^*)$ (linearity) $=\lambda Z^*+(1-\lambda)Z^*=Z^*$.

So $Z(R)=Z^*$ for all $\lambda\in[0,1]$, confirming multiple (infinitely many) optimal points. $\blacksquare$

**Q25.** Solve the following fully: Maximize $Z=2x+3y$ s.t. $x+y\leq30$, $x+2y\leq40$, $x,y\geq0$. Find all corner points, optimal solution, and shadow prices conceptually.

**Solution:**
Corner points:
- $(0,0)$, $(30,0)$ [from $x+y=30$, $y=0$], $(0,20)$ [from $x+2y=40$, $x=0$].
- Intersection: $x+y=30$ and $x+2y=40$: subtract → $y=10$, $x=20$. → $(20,10)$.

| Point | $Z=2x+3y$ |
|-------|-----------|
| $(0,0)$ | 0 |
| $(30,0)$ | 60 |
| $(20,10)$ | 40+30=**70** |
| $(0,20)$ | 60 |

**Maximum $Z=70$ at $(20,10)$.**

**Shadow prices (conceptually):** The shadow price of Constraint 1 ($x+y\leq30$) is the increase in $Z$ per unit increase in RHS=30. If RHS becomes 31: $(x+y=31,x+2y=40)\implies y=9,x=22$, $Z=44+27=71$. Shadow price = $71-70=1$. For Constraint 2: RHS=41: $(x+y=30,x+2y=41)\implies y=11,x=19$, $Z=38+33=71$. Shadow price = 1 each.
