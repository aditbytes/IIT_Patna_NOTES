# Questions & Solutions: 2.4 Advanced LPP — Artificial Variables

> **Topic:** Big-M Method, Two-Phase Method, Artificial Variables, Infeasibility, Special Cases
> **Level:** Easy → Hard

---

## 🟢 Easy (Q1–Q9)

**Q1.** Why are artificial variables needed?

**Solution:**
Artificial variables are needed when an LPP has **equality constraints** ($=$) or **$\geq$ constraints** after subtracting surplus variables — there is no obvious initial BFS. Artificial variables are added to provide an initial identity column so a starting BFS exists.

---

**Q2.** What is the Big-M method?

**Solution:**
The **Big-M method** adds artificial variables $a_i$ with a large penalty coefficient $M$ (where $M\to\infty$) in the objective:

- **Minimization:** Add $+M\cdot a_i$ to the objective function.
- **Maximization:** Add $-M\cdot a_i$ to the objective function.

This forces $a_i=0$ at optimality (otherwise $Z$ is very bad), effectively enforcing the original constraints.

---

**Q3.** What is the Two-Phase method?

**Solution:**
**Phase 1:** Minimize $W = \sum a_i$ (sum of artificial variables) subject to all constraints. If Min $W=0$, a BFS exists (all artificials $=0$).

**Phase 2:** Remove artificial variables, restore original objective, and continue Simplex from the BFS found in Phase 1.

---

**Q4.** What does it mean if Phase 1 ends with $W>0$?

**Solution:**
If Phase 1 ends with $W>0$ (some artificial variable is still positive), the original LPP is **infeasible** — no feasible solution exists.

---

**Q5.** Add an artificial variable to: $3x+2y=12$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
$$3x + 2y + a_1 = 12, \quad a_1 \geq 0$$

Big-M objective (maximize): Add $-Ma_1$.
Phase 1 objective (minimize): $W=a_1$.

---

**Q6.** Convert $2x+y\geq8$ to standard form with surplus and artificial variables.

**Solution:**
$$2x + y - s + a = 8, \quad s, a \geq 0$$

$s$ = surplus variable, $a$ = artificial variable.

---

**Q7.** What should be done with artificial variables after Phase 1?

**Solution:**
After Phase 1 (with optimal $W=0$, all $a_i=0$):
1. If no artificial remains in the basis → remove artificial variable columns and proceed to Phase 2 with original objective.
2. If an artificial remains at value 0 (degenerate) → it may stay in basis but its column is ignored in Phase 2 (its row is treated as a redundant constraint).

---

**Q8.** Write Phase 1 objective for: $x_1+x_2=4$ and $2x_1-x_2\geq3$.

**Solution:**
Add $a_1$ to equality, subtract surplus $s$ and add $a_2$ to $\geq$:
$$x_1+x_2+a_1=4, \quad 2x_1-x_2-s+a_2=3$$

Phase 1: Minimize $W = a_1 + a_2$.

---

**Q9.** If $M$ is not large enough in the Big-M method, what can go wrong?

**Solution:**
If $M$ is insufficiently large, artificial variables may enter the optimal basis with positive values, giving a **spurious solution** that doesn't satisfy the original constraints. $M$ must exceed the maximum possible objective value to guarantee that artificial variables are driven to zero.

## 🟡 Medium (Q10–Q18)

**Q10.** Solve using Big-M: Minimize $Z=2x+3y$ s.t. $x+y=4$, $x+3y\geq6$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Standard form: $x+y+a_1=4$, $x+3y-s+a_2=6$.

Objective (minimize, Big-M): $Z=2x+3y+Ma_1+Ma_2$.

Eliminate $M$ from $Z$-row using initial basis ($a_1,a_2$):
$Z-M(R_1)-M(R_2): Z-M[(1+1)x+(1+3)y+0-s]+M\cdot0=-10M$.
$Z$-row: $[(2-2M)x+(3-4M)y+0\cdot a_1+M\cdot s+0\cdot a_2|-10M]$.

**Tableau 1:**
| $a_1$ | 1 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0 | 4 | 4/1=4 |
| $a_2$ | 1 | 3 | -1 | 0 | 1 | 6 | 6/3=2 ← |
| $Z$ | 2-2M | **3-4M** | M | 0 | 0 | -10M |

Most negative (for large $M$): $3-4M$ (column $y$). Min ratio: 2 ($a_2$ leaves).

$R_2/3$: $[1/3,1,-1/3,0,1/3,2]$. $R_1-R_2$: $[2/3,0,1/3,1,-1/3,2]$. $Z-(3-4M)R_2$: $[(1/3)(1+M),0,(1-M)/3,0,(4M-3)/3,|6-2M]$.

**Tableau 2:**
| $a_1$ | 2/3 | 0 | 1/3 | 1 | -1/3 | 2 | 2/(2/3)=3 |
| $y$ | 1/3 | 1 | -1/3 | 0 | 1/3 | 2 | — |
| $Z$ | (1+M)/3 | 0 | (1-M)/3 | 0 | (4M-3)/3 | 6-2M |

For large $M$: most negative is $(1-M)/3$ → column $s$... but $s$ has entries $1/3>0$ → min ratio: $2/(1/3)=6$. So $a_1$ leaves. After pivot: $a_1=0$, all artificials out.

**Optimal after Phase 2 continuation:** $x=3$, $y=1$, $Z=2(3)+3(1)=\mathbf{9}$.

**Q11.** Solve using Two-Phase: Minimize $Z=x+y$ s.t. $x+2y\geq4$, $x+y=3$, $x,y\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Standard: $x+2y-s_1+a_1=4$, $x+y+a_2=3$.

**Phase 1:** Min $W=a_1+a_2$.
Initial: $a_1=4,a_2=3$, $W=7$.

$W$-row: $W-(R_1+R_2): W-2x-3y+s_1=-7$. $W$-row: $[-2,-3,1,0,0|{-7}]$.

Entering: $y$ (coeff $-3$). Ratios: $4/2=2$, $3/1=3$. $a_1$ leaves.

$R_1/2$: $[1/2,1,-1/2,1/2,0,2]$. $R_2-R_1$: $[1/2,0,1/2,-1/2,1,1]$. $W+3R_1$: $[-1/2,0,-1/2,3/2,0|{-1}]$.

Iteration 2: Entering $x$ (coeff $-1/2$). Ratio: $2/(1/2)=4$, $1/(1/2)=2$. $a_2$ leaves.

$R_2\times2$: $[1,0,1,-1,2,2]$. $R_1-R_1\cdot\frac{1}{2}R_2'$: $[0,1,-1,1,-1,1]$. $W+\frac{1}{2}R_2'$: $[0,0,0,1/2,1|0]$.

$W=0$: Phase 1 complete. BFS: $x=2,y=1$.

**Phase 2:** Min $Z=x+y$. Using basis $\{y,x\}$: already optimal! $Z=2+1=\mathbf{3}$.

---

**Q12.** When does the Two-Phase method have degenerate phase transition?

**Solution:**
When **Phase 1 ends with $W=0$ but an artificial variable remains in the basis at zero value**, the transition is degenerate. The artificial variable occupies a basis position with value 0.

**Handling:** Keep the artificial in the basis but set its objective coefficient to 0 in Phase 2 (it's redundant). Alternatively, try to pivot it out if a non-zero entry exists in its row (outside artificial columns).

---

**Q13.** Compare Big-M and Two-Phase methods. Which is preferred numerically?

**Solution:**
| Feature | Big-M | Two-Phase |
|---------|-------|-----------|
| Phases | Single phase | Two separate phases |
| Numerical | Issues when $M$ is large (round-off) | Numerically more stable |
| Preference | Simple, theoretical | Preferred in practice |
| Termination | May not detect infeasibility cleanly | Infeasibility detected at end of Phase 1 |

**Two-Phase is preferred** for computational implementations due to numerical stability.

**Q14.** Solve using Big-M: Maximize $Z=3x_1+2x_2$ s.t. $2x_1+x_2\leq2$, $3x_1+4x_2\geq12$, $x_1,x_2\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Standard: $2x_1+x_2+s_1=2$, $3x_1+4x_2-s_2+a=12$.

Objective (max): $Z=3x_1+2x_2-Ma$.

$Z$-row after substituting $a=12-3x_1-4x_2+s_2$:
$Z-(-M)(R_2): Z-row: [3-3M, 2-4M... | -12M]$.

For large $M$, most negative: $2-4M$ (column $x_2$). Ratios: $2/1=2$, $12/4=3$. $s_1$ leaves.

After pivoting: $x_2=2,s_1=0$. Check $3x_1+4(2)-s_2+a=12 \implies 3x_1+8=12+s_2-a$, with $x_1=0$: $a=4-s_2$... infeasibility check needed.

Since $3x_1+4x_2\geq12$ but $2x_1+x_2\leq2$ gives max $x_2=2$ when $x_1=0$. Then $4(2)=8<12$. **The LPP is infeasible** — the two constraints cannot both be satisfied.

**Q15.** Show that the initial artificial BFS satisfies the augmented constraints.

**Solution:**
For system $A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}=\mathbf{b}$ (after adding slacks/surplus) with **equality** constraints requiring artificial $\mathbf{a}$:

The augmented system is $A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+I\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{b}$. Setting $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{s}=\mathbf{0}$: $I\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{b} \implies \mathbf{a}=\mathbf{b}\geq\mathbf{0}$.

Since $\mathbf{b}\geq\mathbf{0}$, this is a valid non-negative BFS with basis $\mathbf{a}$. $\blacksquare$

**Q17.** Formulate and set up Big-M tableau for: Maximize $Z=5x_1+3x_2$ s.t. $2x_1+x_2=4$, $x_1+x_2\leq6$, $x_1,x_2\geq0$.

**Solution:**
Standard form: $2x_1+x_2+a_1=4$ (equality gets artificial), $x_1+x_2+s_2=6$ (slack for $\leq$).

Objective (max): $Z=5x_1+3x_2-Ma_1$.

$Z$-row (after adding $M\cdot R_1$ to eliminate $M$ from $a_1$ column): $[5-2M,3-M,0,0,-4M]$.

| $a_1$ | 2 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 4 |
| $s_2$ | 1 | 1 | 0 | 1 | 6 |
| $Z$ | $5-2M$ | $3-M$ | 0 | 0 | $-4M$ |

**Q18.** What is a redundant constraint? How does it appear in Simplex?

**Solution:**
A **redundant constraint** is one that is automatically satisfied whenever all other constraints are — it doesn't limit the feasible region.

In Simplex: After pivoting out an artificial variable, if its row becomes all zeros (except the artificial column), the constraint was redundant. The row can be dropped. If a non-zero artificial remains in the basis at zero, it signals a degenerate solution from redundancy.

**Q20.** Prove that if Phase 1 objective $W>0$ at optimality, the LPP is infeasible.

**Solution:**
In Phase 1, we minimize $W=\sum a_i$ subject to $A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{s}+I\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{b}$, $\mathbf{x},\mathbf{s},\mathbf{a}\geq\mathbf{0}$.

If $W^*>0$ at the minimum of Phase 1, then at any feasible point of Phase 1, $\sum a_i\geq W^*>0$.

A feasible solution to the **original** LPP requires $\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{0}$ (artificials gone), giving $W=0$. But since $W^*>0$ is the minimum, no such point exists.

Therefore the original LPP is **infeasible**. $\blacksquare$

---

**Q21.** For the Big-M tableau, show that the initial $Z$-row must be updated before first iteration.

**Solution:**
The initial basis includes artificial variables $a_i$ with coefficient $M$ in $Z$ (for minimization). For the $Z$-row to correctly reflect non-basic variable costs, we must express $Z$ purely in terms of non-basic variables.

Since $a_i = b_i - \sum_j a_{ij}x_j$ (from constraint rows), substitute into $Z$:
$$Z = \sum_j c_j x_j + M\sum_i a_i = \sum_j c_j x_j + M\sum_i(b_i - \sum_j a_{ij}x_j) = M\sum_i b_i + \sum_j(c_j - M\sum_i a_{ij})x_j$$

This is exactly performing: $Z$-row $\leftarrow Z$-row $- M\times\sum(\text{constraint rows with artificials})$.

Without this update, reduced costs are incorrect and the wrong entering variable would be chosen. $\blacksquare$

**Q22.** Prove: The two-phase and Big-M methods give the same optimal solution (when finite and unique).

**Solution:**
**Sketch:** Both methods find a feasible BFS as starting point for optimizing the true objective. The feasible region and objective function are identical in both methods.

- **Big-M:** Forces $\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{0}$ at optimality. The Simplex path may differ, but the optimal BFS is the same vertex.
- **Two-Phase:** Explicitly finds a feasible BFS (Phase 1), then optimizes (Phase 2).

Both perform Simplex on the same feasible polytope with the same objective → find the same optimal vertex. $\blacksquare$ (Formal proof uses equivalence of LP duals.)
