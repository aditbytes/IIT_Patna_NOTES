# Questions & Solutions: 1.1 Matrices — Fundamentals

> **Topic:** Types of Matrices, Matrix Operations, Rank, Elementary Transformations
> **Level:** Easy → Hard

---

## 🟢 Easy (Q1–Q9)

**Q1.** What is the order of the matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$?

**Solution:**
The matrix has **2 rows** and **3 columns**, so its order is $\boxed{2 \times 3}$.

---

**Q2.** Define a square matrix. Give an example of a $3 \times 3$ square matrix.

**Solution:**
A **square matrix** has equal number of rows and columns ($m = n$).
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}_{3\times3}$$

---

**Q3.** What is a diagonal matrix? Write $\text{diag}(2, 5, 9)$ explicitly.

**Solution:**
A **diagonal matrix** is a square matrix where all off-diagonal elements are zero ($a_{ij} = 0$ for $i \neq j$).
$$\text{diag}(2,5,9) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q4.** Write the identity matrix $I_3$.

**Solution:**
$$I_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q5.** A matrix $A$ is symmetric if $A^T = A$. Is the following matrix symmetric?
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 5 \\ 3 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Solution:**
$$A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 5 \\ 3 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = A$$
Yes, $A^T = A$, so $A$ is **symmetric**. ✓

---

**Q6.** Find $A + B$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$$A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 1+5 & 2+6 \\ 3+7 & 4+8 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q7.** Find $A^T$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
Transpose: swap rows and columns.
$$A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}_{3\times2}$$

---

**Q8.** What is a null (zero) matrix? What is the result of $A + O$ for any matrix $A$?

**Solution:**
A **null matrix** $O$ is a matrix where every element is zero. It is the additive identity:
$$A + O = A \quad \text{for any matrix } A$$

---

**Q9.** If $A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$, identify the type(s) of matrix $A$ is.

**Solution:**
- **Square matrix** (2×2)
- **Diagonal matrix** (off-diagonal elements are 0)
- **Scalar matrix** (all diagonal elements equal: $3 = 3$)
- **Not** an identity matrix (diagonal elements are 3, not 1)
- Can be written as $3I_2$

---

## 🟡 Medium (Q10–Q18)

**Q10.** Compute $AB$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$$AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot2+2\cdot1 & 1\cdot0+2\cdot3 \\ 3\cdot2+4\cdot1 & 3\cdot0+4\cdot3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 10 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q11.** Is matrix multiplication commutative? Give a counter-example.

**Solution:**
Matrix multiplication is **NOT** commutative in general ($AB \neq BA$).

Counter-example: $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$, $B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

$AB = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 10 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$, $BA = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 10 & 14 \end{bmatrix}$. Since $AB \neq BA$, it is not commutative.

---

**Q12.** For $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$, find $A^2 = A \cdot A$.

**Solution:**
$$A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4+4 & 8+12 \\ 2+3 & 4+9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 & 20 \\ 5 & 13 \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q13.** State and explain the three types of elementary row operations.

**Solution:**
1. **$R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$** — Swap two rows
2. **$R_i \leftarrow kR_i$** — Multiply a row by a non-zero scalar $k$
3. **$R_i \leftarrow R_i + kR_j$** — Add $k$ times row $j$ to row $i$

These operations do not change the rank of a matrix.

---

**Q14.** Find the rank of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1,\quad R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_1$$
$$\to \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3: \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
Two non-zero rows → $\rho(A) = \boxed{2}$

---

**Q15.** Reduce $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \\ 5 & 6 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ to row echelon form.

**Solution:**
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 5R_1$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & -4 & -15 \end{bmatrix}$$
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + 4R_2$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \leftarrow \text{Row Echelon Form}$$

---

**Q16.** Verify whether $A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 3 & -1 \\ -3 & 0 & 2 \\ 1 & -2 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ is skew-symmetric.

**Solution:**
$$A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -3 & 1 \\ 3 & 0 & -2 \\ -1 & 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = -A \checkmark$$
Since $A^T = -A$, $A$ is **skew-symmetric**. ✓

---

**Q17.** Define the normal form of a matrix. What does the normal form of a rank-$r$ matrix look like?

**Solution:**
The **normal form** (canonical form) of a matrix of rank $r$ is:
$$\begin{bmatrix} I_r & O \\ O & O \end{bmatrix}$$
where $I_r$ is the $r \times r$ identity matrix and the remaining blocks are zero matrices. Every matrix can be reduced to this form using elementary row and column operations.

---

**Q18.** If $A$ is a $3 \times 4$ matrix, what are the possible values of $\rho(A)$?

**Solution:**
For an $m \times n$ matrix, $\rho(A) \leq \min(m, n)$.
Here $\min(3, 4) = 3$, so $\rho(A) \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$.

## 🔴 Hard (Q19–Q25)

**Q19.** Reduce $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 & 3 & 4 \\ 3 & 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$ to normal form and find its rank.

**Solution:**
$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_1$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & -3 & 5 & 2 \\ 0 & -3 & 5 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & -3 & 5 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
Two non-zero rows → $\rho(A) = \boxed{2}$. Normal form: $\begin{bmatrix} I_2 & O \\ O & O \end{bmatrix}$ in $3\times4$ format.

---

**Q20.** Prove that $\rho(AB) \leq \min(\rho(A), \rho(B))$.

**Solution:**
- Every column of $AB$ is a linear combination of columns of $A$ (since $AB = A[b_1|b_2|\cdots|b_n]$). Hence the column space of $AB \subseteq$ column space of $A$, so $\rho(AB) \leq \rho(A)$.
- Similarly, every row of $AB$ is a linear combination of rows of $B$. Hence $\rho(AB) \leq \rho(B)$.
- Combining: $\rho(AB) \leq \min(\rho(A), \rho(B))$. $\blacksquare$

---

**Q21.** Show that $\rho(A) = \rho(A^T)$.

**Solution:**
The row rank of $A$ = the column rank of $A$ (fundamental theorem of linear algebra). The row rank of $A$ equals the column rank of $A^T$ (rows of $A$ = columns of $A^T$). Since column rank = row rank, $\rho(A) = \rho(A^T)$. $\blacksquare$

---

**Q22.** Show $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ is idempotent. Find all $2\times2$ idempotent diagonal matrices.

**Solution:**
$A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = A$ ✓

For $D = \text{diag}(d_1, d_2)$: $D^2 = D$ requires $d_i^2 = d_i$, so $d_i(d_i-1)=0$, meaning $d_i \in \{0,1\}$.

All idempotent diagonal $2\times2$ matrices: $\text{diag}(0,0),\ \text{diag}(1,0),\ \text{diag}(0,1),\ \text{diag}(1,1)$.

---

**Q23.** Prove: if $A$ is both symmetric and skew-symmetric, then $A = O$.

**Solution:**
Symmetric: $A^T = A$. Skew-symmetric: $A^T = -A$.
Therefore $A = A^T = -A$, which gives $2A = O$, hence $A = O$. $\blacksquare$

---

**Q24.** For $A$ of order $m \times n$, prove the maximum rank is $\min(m,n)$.

**Solution:**
- Rank = dimension of row space = dimension of column space.
- The row space is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ (dimension $\leq n$), so $\rho(A) \leq n$.
- The column space is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$ (dimension $\leq m$), so $\rho(A) \leq m$.
- Combining: $\rho(A) \leq \min(m,n)$. $\blacksquare$

---

**Q25.** Find the rank of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & -3 & -6 \\ 3 & -3 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 3 & -3 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & -3 & -6 \end{bmatrix}$$
$R_2 \leftarrow R_2-3R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3-2R_1$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & -6 & -2 & -4 \\ 0 & -1 & -5 & -10 \end{bmatrix}$$
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \tfrac{1}{6}R_2$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & -6 & -2 & -4 \\ 0 & 0 & -\tfrac{13}{3} & -\tfrac{26}{3} \end{bmatrix}$$
Three non-zero rows → $\rho(A) = \boxed{3}$.

Normal form: $\begin{bmatrix} I_3 & O \end{bmatrix}$ (3×4 matrix with $I_3$ on the left).
