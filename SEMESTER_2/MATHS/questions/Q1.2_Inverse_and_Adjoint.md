# Questions & Solutions: 1.2 Inverse & Adjoint of Matrices

> **Topic:** Minors, Cofactors, Determinants, Adjoint, Inverse, Cramer's Rule
> **Level:** Easy → Hard

---

## 🟢 Easy (Q1–Q9)

**Q1.** Find $M_{11}$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
Delete row 1 and column 1: the remaining element is $4$.
$$M_{11} = |[4]| = 4$$

---

**Q2.** Compute the determinant of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$$|A| = 5 \cdot 4 - 3 \cdot 2 = 20 - 6 = \boxed{14}$$

---

**Q3.** State the relationship: cofactor $C_{ij}$ in terms of minor $M_{ij}$.

**Solution:**
$$C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \cdot M_{ij}$$
The sign follows the checkerboard pattern:
$\begin{bmatrix} + & - & + \\ - & + & - \\ + & - & + \end{bmatrix}$

---

**Q4.** If $|A| = 0$, what can you conclude about the invertibility of $A$?

**Solution:**
$A$ is **singular** (non-invertible). Since $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{|A|}\text{adj}(A)$, and $|A| = 0$, the inverse does not exist.

---

**Q5.** Compute $|A|$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
For a diagonal matrix, the determinant is the product of diagonal entries:
$$|A| = 1 \times 2 \times 3 = \boxed{6}$$

---

**Q6.** State any three properties of determinants.

**Solution:**
1. $|A^T| = |A|$
2. $|AB| = |A| \cdot |B|$
3. If two rows (or columns) are identical, $|A| = 0$
4. $|kA| = k^n |A|$ for an $n \times n$ matrix (also valid answer)

---

**Q7.** For $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$, write $\text{adj}(A)$.

**Solution:**
For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, the adjoint is obtained by swapping diagonal elements and negating off-diagonal elements:
$$\text{adj}(A) = \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q8.** Write the formula for $A^{-1}$ in terms of adjoint and determinant.

**Solution:**
$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{|A|} \cdot \text{adj}(A), \quad \text{provided } |A| \neq 0$$

---

**Q9.** If $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$, find $A^{-1}$ by inspection.

**Solution:**
For a diagonal matrix, $A^{-1} = \text{diag}(1/d_1, 1/d_2)$:
$$A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/5 \end{bmatrix}$$

## 🟡 Medium (Q10–Q18)

**Q10.** Compute all 9 cofactors of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$$C_{11} = (+1)\begin{vmatrix}4&5\\0&2\end{vmatrix}=8,\quad C_{12}=(-1)\begin{vmatrix}0&5\\1&2\end{vmatrix}=5,\quad C_{13}=(+1)\begin{vmatrix}0&4\\1&0\end{vmatrix}=-4$$
$$C_{21}=(-1)\begin{vmatrix}2&3\\0&2\end{vmatrix}=-4,\quad C_{22}=(+1)\begin{vmatrix}1&3\\1&2\end{vmatrix}=-1,\quad C_{23}=(-1)\begin{vmatrix}1&2\\1&0\end{vmatrix}=2$$
$$C_{31}=(+1)\begin{vmatrix}2&3\\4&5\end{vmatrix}=-2,\quad C_{32}=(-1)\begin{vmatrix}1&3\\0&5\end{vmatrix}=-5,\quad C_{33}=(+1)\begin{vmatrix}1&2\\0&4\end{vmatrix}=4$$

---

**Q11.** Find $|A|$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
Expanding along row 1:
$$|A| = 2\begin{vmatrix}4&5\\0&2\end{vmatrix} - 1\begin{vmatrix}0&5\\1&2\end{vmatrix} + 3\begin{vmatrix}0&4\\1&0\end{vmatrix}$$
$$= 2(8-0) - 1(0-5) + 3(0-4) = 16 + 5 - 12 = \boxed{9}$$

---

**Q12.** Find $\text{adj}(A)$ and $A^{-1}$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$|A| = 4-6 = -2$.
$$\text{adj}(A) = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-2}\begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3/2 & -1/2 \end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q13.** Verify $A \cdot \text{adj}(A) = |A| \cdot I$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$|A| = 8 - 3 = 5$. $\text{adj}(A) = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.
$$A \cdot \text{adj}(A) = \begin{bmatrix}2&3\\1&4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4&-3\\-1&2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}8-3&-6+6\\4-4&-3+8\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}5&0\\0&5\end{bmatrix} = 5I \checkmark$$

---

**Q14.** Using Cramer's Rule, solve: $2x + y = 5$, $3x - 2y = 4$.

**Solution:**
$D = \begin{vmatrix}2&1\\3&-2\end{vmatrix} = -4-3 = -7$

$D_x = \begin{vmatrix}5&1\\4&-2\end{vmatrix} = -10-4 = -14$, $\quad D_y = \begin{vmatrix}2&5\\3&4\end{vmatrix} = 8-15 = -7$

$$x = \frac{D_x}{D} = \frac{-14}{-7} = 2, \qquad y = \frac{D_y}{D} = \frac{-7}{-7} = 1$$

---

**Q15.** Use elementary row operations to find $A^{-1}$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 7 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
Augment: $[A|I] = \begin{bmatrix}1&2&|&1&0\\3&7&|&0&1\end{bmatrix}$

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 3R_1$: $\begin{bmatrix}1&2&|&1&0\\0&1&|&-3&1\end{bmatrix}$

$R_1 \leftarrow R_1 - 2R_2$: $\begin{bmatrix}1&0&|&7&-2\\0&1&|&-3&1\end{bmatrix}$

$$A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}7 & -2 \\ -3 & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

---

**Q16.** Prove $|A^{-1}| = \dfrac{1}{|A|}$.

**Solution:**
Since $A \cdot A^{-1} = I$, taking determinants on both sides:
$$|A| \cdot |A^{-1}| = |I| = 1 \implies |A^{-1}| = \frac{1}{|A|}. \quad \blacksquare$$

---

**Q17.** Prove $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$.

**Solution:**
$(B^{-1}A^{-1})(AB) = B^{-1}(A^{-1}A)B = B^{-1}IB = B^{-1}B = I$

$(AB)(B^{-1}A^{-1}) = A(BB^{-1})A^{-1} = AIA^{-1} = AA^{-1} = I$

Since both products give $I$, $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$. $\blacksquare$

---

**Q18.** Find $A^{-1}$ using ERO for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
$[A|I]$, $R_2\leftarrow R_2-2R_1$:
$\begin{bmatrix}1&0&1&|&1&0&0\\0&1&-2&|&-2&1&0\\0&1&1&|&0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$

$R_3\leftarrow R_3-R_2$: $\begin{bmatrix}1&0&1&|&1&0&0\\0&1&-2&|&-2&1&0\\0&0&3&|&2&-1&1\end{bmatrix}$

$R_3\leftarrow R_3/3$, then $R_2\leftarrow R_2+2R_3$, $R_1\leftarrow R_1-R_3$:
$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}1&1&-1\\-2&1&2\\2&-1&1\end{bmatrix}$$

## 🔴 Hard (Q19–Q25)

**Q19.** Using Cramer's Rule, solve: $x+y+z=6$, $2x-y+z=3$, $x+2y-z=2$.

**Solution:**
$D = \begin{vmatrix}1&1&1\\2&-1&1\\1&2&-1\end{vmatrix}$

Expanding: $D = 1(1-2)-1(-2-1)+1(4+1) = -1+3+5 = 7$

$D_x = \begin{vmatrix}6&1&1\\3&-1&1\\2&2&-1\end{vmatrix} = 6(1-2)-1(-3-2)+1(6+2) = -6+5+8 = 7$

$D_y = \begin{vmatrix}1&6&1\\2&3&1\\1&2&-1\end{vmatrix} = 1(-3-2)-6(-2-1)+1(4-3) = -5+18+1 = 14$

$D_z = \begin{vmatrix}1&1&6\\2&-1&3\\1&2&2\end{vmatrix} = 1(-2-6)-1(4-3)+6(4+1) = -8-1+30 = 21$

$$x = 7/7 = 1,\quad y = 14/7 = 2,\quad z = 21/7 = 3$$

**Q20.** Show $A = \begin{bmatrix}1&1&1\\1&2&3\\1&4&9\end{bmatrix}$ is invertible and find $A^{-1}$.

**Solution:**
$|A| = 1(18-12)-1(9-3)+1(4-2) = 6-6+2 = 2 \neq 0$ → invertible.

Using cofactors:
$C_{11}=6,\ C_{12}=-6,\ C_{13}=2,\ C_{21}=-5,\ C_{22}=8,\ C_{23}=-3,\ C_{31}=1,\ C_{32}=-2,\ C_{33}=1$

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix}6&-5&1\\-6&8&-2\\2&-3&1\end{bmatrix}$$

**Q21.** For $A = \begin{bmatrix}0&1&2\\1&2&3\\3&1&1\end{bmatrix}$, find $\text{adj}(A)$ and verify $A\cdot\text{adj}(A) = |A|\cdot I$.

**Solution:**
$|A| = 0(2-3)-1(1-9)+2(1-6) = 0+8-10 = -2$

Cofactors: $C_{11}=-1,\ C_{12}=8,\ C_{13}=-5,\ C_{21}=1,\ C_{22}=-6,\ C_{23}=3,\ C_{31}=-1,\ C_{32}=2,\ C_{33}=-1$

$\text{adj}(A) = [C_{ij}]^T = \begin{bmatrix}-1&1&-1\\8&-6&2\\-5&3&-1\end{bmatrix}$

Verification: $A \cdot \text{adj}(A) = -2I$ ✓

**Q22.** Without expanding, evaluate $\begin{vmatrix}a+b&b+c&c+a\\b+c&c+a&a+b\\c+a&a+b&b+c\end{vmatrix}$.

**Solution:**
Let $s = a+b+c$. Each row sums to $2s$. Add all columns to column 1: each entry becomes $2(a+b+c) = 2s$.

Factor $2s$ from column 1, then subtract to show linear dependence... or note: $R_1+R_2+R_3$ shows row 1 = Row 2 + Row 3 relationship. Actually:

$C_1 \leftarrow C_1+C_2+C_3$: each entry becomes $2(a+b+c)$. Factor out $2(a+b+c)$, then $C_1$ is all 1s. Then $C_2\leftarrow C_2-C_1(b+c)$ etc. leads to two identical rows.

The determinant equals $\boxed{2(a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc)}$.

**Q23.** Prove $\text{adj}(\text{adj}(A)) = |A|^{n-2}A$ for $n\times n$ matrix, $n\geq 2$.

**Solution:**
We know $A \cdot \text{adj}(A) = |A|I$. Also $|\text{adj}(A)| = |A|^{n-1}$.

Applying the adjoint formula to $\text{adj}(A)$:
$\text{adj}(A) \cdot \text{adj}(\text{adj}(A)) = |\text{adj}(A)| \cdot I = |A|^{n-1}I$

From $A \cdot \text{adj}(A) = |A|I$, multiply both sides by $A^{-1}$ (if $|A|\neq 0$):
$\text{adj}(A) = |A| A^{-1}$

Then $\text{adj}(\text{adj}(A)) = |A|^{n-1}(\text{adj}(A))^{-1} = |A|^{n-1}\cdot\frac{1}{|A|}A = |A|^{n-2}A$. $\blacksquare$

**Q25.** Prove that the determinant of an orthogonal matrix is $\pm 1$.

**Solution:**
An orthogonal matrix satisfies $AA^T = I$.

Taking determinants:
$$|AA^T| = |I| = 1$$
$$|A| \cdot |A^T| = 1$$
$$|A| \cdot |A| = 1 \quad (\text{since } |A^T| = |A|)$$
$$|A|^2 = 1 \implies |A| = \pm 1. \quad \blacksquare$$
