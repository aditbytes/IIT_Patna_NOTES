## What are Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors? (Layman's Explanation)

Imagine you apply a transformation (like stretching or rotating) to a vector. Most vectors change their direction. But some **special vectors only get scaled** (stretched or shrunk) â€” they keep pointing in the **same direction** (or exactly opposite). These special vectors are called **eigenvectors**, and the scaling factor is called the **eigenvalue**.

Think of it like a wind blowing on flags. Most flags flap in random directions, but some flags are aligned perfectly with the wind â€” they just stretch longer or shorter. Those flags are eigenvectors.

---

## Formal Definition

Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ square matrix. A scalar $\lambda$ is an **eigenvalue** of $A$, and a non-zero vector $X$ is the corresponding **eigenvector**, if:

$$
\boxed{AX = \lambda X}
$$

This can be rewritten as:
$$
AX - \lambda X = 0 \implies (A - \lambda I)X = 0
$$

For **non-trivial solutions** (eigenvectors $X \neq 0$), we need:

$$
\boxed{|A - \lambda I| = 0}
$$

This is called the **characteristic equation**.

---

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Find Eigenvalues
Solve $|A - \lambda I| = 0$ (characteristic equation) for $\lambda$.

### Step 2: Find Eigenvectors
For each eigenvalue $\lambda$, solve $(A - \lambda I)X = 0$ to find the eigenvectors.

---

## Examples

### Example 1: 2Ă—2 Matrix

Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 1: Characteristic Equation**

$$
|A - \lambda I| = \begin{vmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{vmatrix} = 0
$$

$(4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = 0$

$12 - 7\lambda + \lambda^2 - 2 = 0$

$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$

$(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$

**Eigenvalues:** $\lambda_1 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = 2$

**Step 2: Eigenvectors**

**For $\lambda_1 = 5$:**

$(A - 5I)X = 0$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$-x_1 + x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2 = x_1$

Let $x_1 = t$: $X_1 = t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Eigenvector for $\lambda = 5$:** $X_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ (or any scalar multiple)

**For $\lambda_2 = 2$:**

$(A - 2I)X = 0$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$2x_1 + x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2 = -2x_1$

Let $x_1 = t$: $X_2 = t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$

**Eigenvector for $\lambda = 2$:** $X_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}$

---

### Example 2: 3Ă—3 Matrix

Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

**Step 1: Characteristic Equation**

$$
|A - \lambda I| = \begin{vmatrix} 2-\lambda & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2-\lambda & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 2-\lambda \end{vmatrix} = 0
$$

Expanding along $R_2$: $(2-\lambda)[(2-\lambda)^2 - 1] = 0$

$(2-\lambda)(4 - 4\lambda + \lambda^2 - 1) = 0$

$(2-\lambda)(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3) = 0$

$(2-\lambda)(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$

**Eigenvalues:** $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 2$, $\lambda_3 = 3$

**Step 2: Eigenvectors**

**For $\lambda = 1$:** $(A - I)X = 0$

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$x + z = 0$, $y = 0$. Let $z = t$: $X_1 = t\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

**For $\lambda = 2$:** $(A - 2I)X = 0$

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$z = 0$, $x = 0$, $y$ is free. $X_2 = t\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

**For $\lambda = 3$:** $(A - 3I)X = 0$

$$
\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

$-x + z = 0$, $y = 0$. Let $z = t$: $X_3 = t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

---

### Example 3: Repeated Eigenvalues

Find eigenvalues of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

$|A - \lambda I| = (2-\lambda)^2 = 0$

$\lambda = 2$ (repeated, **algebraic multiplicity** = 2)

$(A - 2I)X = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

$y = 0$, $x$ is free. $X = t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

Only **one linearly independent eigenvector** (**geometric multiplicity** = 1).

## Characteristic Polynomial

The polynomial $p(\lambda) = |A - \lambda I|$ is called the **characteristic polynomial** of $A$.

For an $n \times n$ matrix:
$$
p(\lambda) = (-1)^n \lambda^n + (-1)^{n-1}(\text{tr}(A))\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + |A|
$$

### Properties
- Sum of eigenvalues = **trace** of $A$: $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n = \text{tr}(A)$
- Product of eigenvalues = **determinant** of $A$: $\lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_n = |A|$

---

## Properties of Eigenvalues

| Property | Statement |
|----------|-----------|
| Eigenvalues of $A^T$ | Same as eigenvalues of $A$ |
| Eigenvalues of $kA$ | $k\lambda_1, k\lambda_2, \ldots$ |
| Eigenvalues of $A^k$ | $\lambda_1^k, \lambda_2^k, \ldots$ |
| Eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$ | $\frac{1}{\lambda_1}, \frac{1}{\lambda_2}, \ldots$ |
| Eigenvalues of $A + kI$ | $\lambda_1 + k, \lambda_2 + k, \ldots$ |
| Eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$ | If $\lambda$ is eigenvalue of $A$, then $1/\lambda$ is eigenvalue of $A^{-1}$ |
| Triangular/Diagonal matrix | Eigenvalues = diagonal entries |
| Symmetric matrix | All eigenvalues are **real** |
| Skew-symmetric matrix | Eigenvalues are **zero or purely imaginary** |
| Orthogonal matrix | $|\lambda| = 1$ |
| Idempotent matrix ($A^2 = A$) | Eigenvalues are **0 or 1** |
| Nilpotent matrix | All eigenvalues are **0** |
| Involutory matrix ($A^2 = I$) | Eigenvalues are **1 or -1** |

## Cayley-Hamilton Theorem (VERY IMPORTANT)

### Statement
Every square matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation.

If the characteristic polynomial of $A$ is:
$$
p(\lambda) = \lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + c_1\lambda + c_0 = 0
$$

Then:
$$
\boxed{A^n + c_{n-1}A^{n-1} + \cdots + c_1 A + c_0 I = O}
$$

(Replace $\lambda$ with $A$ and the constant term gets multiplied by $I$.)

### Example 1: Verify Cayley-Hamilton for 2Ă—2

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$

Characteristic equation: $\lambda^2 - 5\lambda - 2 = 0$

By Cayley-Hamilton: $A^2 - 5A - 2I = O$

**Verify:**
$A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 10 \\ 15 & 22 \end{bmatrix}$

$A^2 - 5A - 2I = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 10 \\ 15 & 22 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 10 \\ 15 & 20 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = O$ âś“

### Example 2: Find $A^{-1}$ using Cayley-Hamilton

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

Characteristic equation: $\lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 5 = 0$

By Cayley-Hamilton: $A^2 - 5A + 5I = O$

Multiply both sides by $A^{-1}$:
$A - 5I + 5A^{-1} = O$

$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}(5I - A) = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

### Example 3: Find $A^3$ using Cayley-Hamilton

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$

From C-H: $A^2 = 5A + 2I$

$A^3 = A \cdot A^2 = A(5A + 2I) = 5A^2 + 2A = 5(5A + 2I) + 2A = 27A + 10I$

$= 27\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} + 10\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 37 & 54 \\ 81 & 118 \end{bmatrix}$

## Minimal Polynomial

### Definition
The **minimal polynomial** $m(\lambda)$ of $A$ is the polynomial of **lowest degree** such that $m(A) = O$.

### Properties
1. The minimal polynomial **divides** the characteristic polynomial
2. Both polynomials have the **same roots** (but possibly different multiplicities)
3. Degree of minimal polynomial â‰¤ degree of characteristic polynomial

### Example
$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = 2I$

Characteristic polynomial: $(\lambda - 2)^2 = 0$

But $(A - 2I) = O$ â†’ minimal polynomial is $(\lambda - 2)$ (degree 1, not 2).

---

## Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

### Algebraic Multiplicity (AM)
The number of times an eigenvalue appears as a root of the characteristic polynomial.

### Geometric Multiplicity (GM)
The number of linearly independent eigenvectors for that eigenvalue = $n - \text{rank}(A - \lambda I)$.

### Key Relationship
$$
1 \leq \text{GM}(\lambda) \leq \text{AM}(\lambda)
$$

### Example
$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$

Characteristic polynomial: $(2-\lambda)^2(3-\lambda) = 0$

- $\lambda = 2$: AM = 2, GM = 1 (since rank$(A - 2I)$ = 2, so GM = 3 - 2 = 1)
- $\lambda = 3$: AM = 1, GM = 1

---

## Eigenspaces

### Definition
The **eigenspace** corresponding to eigenvalue $\lambda$ is:

$$
E_\lambda = \ker(A - \lambda I) = \{X : (A - \lambda I)X = 0\}
$$

It is the **set of all eigenvectors** for $\lambda$, plus the zero vector.

$E_\lambda$ is a **subspace** of $\mathbb{R}^n$, and $\dim(E_\lambda)$ = geometric multiplicity of $\lambda$.

**Q4.** Show that eigenvalues of a real symmetric matrix are real.

**Proof:**
Let $AX = \lambda X$ where $X \neq 0$.

Take conjugate transpose: $\bar{X}^T A^T = \bar{\lambda} \bar{X}^T$ (since $A$ is real and symmetric, $A^T = A$)

So: $\bar{X}^T A = \bar{\lambda} \bar{X}^T$

Multiply original by $\bar{X}^T$ from left: $\bar{X}^T A X = \lambda \bar{X}^T X$

From the conjugate equation, multiply by $X$ from right: $\bar{X}^T A X = \bar{\lambda} \bar{X}^T X$

Therefore: $\lambda \bar{X}^T X = \bar{\lambda} \bar{X}^T X$

Since $\bar{X}^T X = |x_1|^2 + |x_2|^2 + \cdots > 0$ (as $X \neq 0$):

$\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$, so $\lambda$ is **real**. âś“
