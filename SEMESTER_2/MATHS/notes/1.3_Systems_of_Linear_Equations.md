## What is a System of Linear Equations?

A **system of linear equations** is a collection of equations where each equation is a **first-degree** (linear) equation in the unknowns.

**Example (3 equations, 3 unknowns):**
$$
x + 2y + 3z = 9
$$
$$
2x + y + z = 7
$$
$$
x + y + 2z = 8
$$

### Matrix Form: $AX = B$

$$
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}}_{A} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}}_{X} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 9 \\ 7 \\ 8 \end{bmatrix}}_{B}
$$

- $A$ = coefficient matrix
- $X$ = column of unknowns
- $B$ = column of constants

**Augmented Matrix:** $[A|B]$ — the coefficient matrix with $B$ appended as the last column.

$$
[A|B] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 9 \\ 2 & 1 & 1 & 7 \\ 1 & 1 & 2 & 8 \end{array}\right]
$$

---

## Types of Systems

### 1. Homogeneous System: $AX = 0$

The right-hand side is all zeros.

$$
x + 2y + 3z = 0
$$
$$
2x + y + z = 0
$$
$$
x + y + 2z = 0
$$

**Key fact:** A homogeneous system **always has at least one solution** — the **trivial solution** $x = y = z = 0$.

### 2. Non-Homogeneous System: $AX = B$ (where $B \neq 0$)

The right-hand side has non-zero values. May or may not have a solution.

---

## Consistency of a System

| Condition | System is... | Solutions |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| System has at least one solution | **Consistent** | One or infinitely many |
| System has no solution | **Inconsistent** | None |

---

## Rank Method (Rouché-Capelli Theorem)

This is the **most important theorem** for determining whether a system has solutions.

### Theorem

For a system $AX = B$ with $n$ unknowns:

| Condition | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| $\rho(A) \neq \rho([A|B])$ | **No solution** (inconsistent) |
| $\rho(A) = \rho([A|B]) = n$ | **Unique solution** |
| $\rho(A) = \rho([A|B]) = r < n$ | **Infinitely many solutions** (with $n - r$ free variables) |

### How to Apply
1. Form the augmented matrix $[A|B]$
2. Reduce to echelon form using row operations
3. Find $\rho(A)$ and $\rho([A|B])$
4. Apply the theorem above

## Non-Homogeneous Systems: $AX = B$

### Example 1: Unique Solution

Solve:
$$
x + y + z = 6
$$
$$
x + 2y + 3z = 14
$$
$$
x + 4y + 9z = 36
$$

**Step 1:** Form augmented matrix and reduce:

$$
[A|B] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 14 \\ 1 & 4 & 9 & 36 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_2 \to R_2 - R_1$, $R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 8 \\ 0 & 3 & 8 & 30 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_3 \to R_3 - 3R_2$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 8 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 6 \end{array}\right]
$$

**Step 2:** $\rho(A) = 3$, $\rho([A|B]) = 3$, $n = 3$.

Since $\rho(A) = \rho([A|B]) = n = 3$ → **Unique solution**.

**Step 3: Back substitution:**

From Row 3: $2z = 6 \Rightarrow z = 3$

From Row 2: $y + 2(3) = 8 \Rightarrow y = 2$

From Row 1: $x + 2 + 3 = 6 \Rightarrow x = 1$

**Solution:** $x = 1, y = 2, z = 3$

---

### Example 2: Infinitely Many Solutions

Solve:
$$
x + 2y + 3z = 1
$$
$$
2x + 4y + 6z = 2
$$
$$
3x + 6y + 9z = 3
$$

$$
[A|B] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 & 2 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 & 3 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \to R_3 - 3R_1$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$\rho(A) = 1$, $\rho([A|B]) = 1$, $n = 3$.

Since $\rho(A) = \rho([A|B]) = 1 < 3$ → **Infinitely many solutions** with $3 - 1 = 2$ free variables.

Let $y = s$, $z = t$ (free variables). Then:

$$
x = 1 - 2s - 3t
$$

**Solution:** $x = 1 - 2s - 3t, \quad y = s, \quad z = t$ where $s, t \in \mathbb{R}$.

---

### Example 3: No Solution (Inconsistent)

Solve:
$$
x + y + z = 1
$$
$$
x + y + z = 2
$$
$$
x + 2y + 3z = 3
$$

$$
[A|B] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 3 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_2 \to R_2 - R_1$, $R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 2 \end{array}\right]
$$

Row 2 says: $0x + 0y + 0z = 1$, which is **impossible**.

$\rho(A) = 2$, $\rho([A|B]) = 3$.

Since $\rho(A) \neq \rho([A|B])$ → **No solution** (inconsistent).

## Homogeneous Systems: $AX = 0$

### Key Properties
1. **Always consistent** (trivial solution $X = 0$ always exists)
2. If $\rho(A) = n$ (number of unknowns) → **Only trivial solution** ($X = 0$)
3. If $\rho(A) = r < n$ → **Non-trivial solutions** exist (infinitely many, with $n - r$ free variables)

### Special Case: Square Coefficient Matrix ($m = n$)
- If $|A| \neq 0$ → Only trivial solution
- If $|A| = 0$ → Non-trivial solutions exist

### Example 1: Only Trivial Solution

Solve:
$$
x + y + z = 0
$$
$$
x + 2y + 3z = 0
$$
$$
x + 4y + 9z = 0
$$

$$
[A|0] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 0 \\ 1 & 4 & 9 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_2 \to R_2 - R_1$, $R_3 \to R_3 - R_1$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 8 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_3 \to R_3 - 3R_2$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$\rho(A) = 3 = n$ → **Only trivial solution**: $x = 0, y = 0, z = 0$.

---

### Example 2: Non-Trivial Solutions

Solve:
$$
x + 2y + 3z = 0
$$
$$
2x + 3y + 4z = 0
$$
$$
3x + 5y + 7z = 0
$$

$$
[A|0] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 \\ 3 & 5 & 7 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_2 \to R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \to R_3 - 3R_1$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$R_3 \to R_3 - R_2$:

$$
\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right]
$$

$\rho(A) = 2 < n = 3$ → **Non-trivial solutions** exist. Free variables = $3 - 2 = 1$.

Let $z = t$ (free variable).

From Row 2: $-y - 2t = 0 \Rightarrow y = -2t$

From Row 1: $x + 2(-2t) + 3t = 0 \Rightarrow x = t$

**Solution:** $x = t, \quad y = -2t, \quad z = t$ where $t \in \mathbb{R}$

Or in vector form: $X = t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

---

### Example 3: Find values of $k$ for non-trivial solutions

$$
kx + y + z = 0
$$
$$
x + ky + z = 0
$$
$$
x + y + kz = 0
$$

For non-trivial solutions, $|A| = 0$:

$$
|A| = \begin{vmatrix} k & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & k & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & k \end{vmatrix} = 0
$$

Expand: $k(k^2-1) - 1(k-1) + 1(1-k) = 0$

$k^3 - k - k + 1 + 1 - k = 0$

$k^3 - 3k + 2 = 0$

$(k-1)^2(k+2) = 0$

$k = 1$ or $k = -2$

---

## Relationship Between Homogeneous and Non-Homogeneous

If $AX = B$ has a particular solution $X_p$, and the homogeneous system $AX = 0$ has general solution $X_h$, then:

$$
\text{General solution of } AX = B = X_p + X_h
$$

This is similar to the concept in differential equations — **particular + complementary solution**.

## Summary Table

| System | Condition | Result |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| $AX = B$ | $\rho(A) \neq \rho([A\|B])$ | No solution |
| $AX = B$ | $\rho(A) = \rho([A\|B]) = n$ | Unique solution |
| $AX = B$ | $\rho(A) = \rho([A\|B]) = r < n$ | Infinite solutions ($n-r$ free vars) |
| $AX = 0$ | $\rho(A) = n$ | Only trivial solution ($X = 0$) |
| $AX = 0$ | $\rho(A) < n$ | Non-trivial solutions exist |
| $AX = 0$ (square) | $\|A\| \neq 0$ | Only trivial solution |
| $AX = 0$ (square) | $\|A\| = 0$ | Non-trivial solutions |

## Practice Questions

**Q1.** Investigate the consistency: $x + y + z = 3$, $x + 2y + 3z = 4$, $2x + 3y + 4z = 7$.

**Solution:**
$[A|B] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 2 & 3 & 4 & 7 \end{array}\right]$

$R_2 \to R_2 - R_1$, $R_3 \to R_3 - 2R_1$:

$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \end{array}\right]$

$R_3 \to R_3 - R_2$: $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right]$

$\rho(A) = 2 = \rho([A|B])$, $n = 3$ → Infinitely many solutions.

Let $z = t$: $y = 1 - 2t$, $x = 2 + t$.

---

**Q2.** Solve: $2x + y - z = 0$, $x - y + z = 0$, $3x + 3y - 3z = 0$.

**Solution:**
$R_3 \to R_3 - 3R_2$: After reduction, $\rho(A) = 2$, $n = 3$.

Let $z = t$: From echelon form, $y = t$, $x = 0$.

Solution: $X = t\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

---

**Q3.** For what value of $\lambda$, the system $x + y + z = 1$, $x + 2y + 4z = \lambda$, $x + 4y + 10z = \lambda^2$ is consistent?

**Solution:**
$[A|B] \to \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 & \lambda-1 \\ 0 & 3 & 9 & \lambda^2-1 \end{array}\right]$

$R_3 \to R_3 - 3R_2$: $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 & \lambda-1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda^2 - 3\lambda + 2 \end{array}\right]$

For consistency: $\lambda^2 - 3\lambda + 2 = 0 \Rightarrow (\lambda-1)(\lambda-2) = 0$

$\lambda = 1$ or $\lambda = 2$.

## Key Formulas to Remember

| Formula | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| $\rho(A) = \rho([A\|B]) = n$ | Unique solution |
| $\rho(A) = \rho([A\|B]) < n$ | Infinite solutions |
| $\rho(A) \neq \rho([A\|B])$ | No solution |
| Free variables = $n - \rho(A)$ | Number of parameters in solution |
| $\|A\| = 0$ for homogeneous | Non-trivial solutions exist |
